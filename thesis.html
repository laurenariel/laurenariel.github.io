WHEN IT IS OVER WE WILL CELEBRATE

The contents of this project were created during a dark period of world history. The coronavirus pandemic has become infamous unto itself, with its death toll alone enough to make one eternally somber. It is a time of deep mourning, suffering, and loss. But it has also been a time of reconciliation, of healing and of community, even at its smallest moments. This book was created with that last point in mind. Its contents were created in a space of true sadness: of fear, uncertainty, and death. But the contents themselves tell of the human aspects to even an inhumane global pandemic, of the significance of family, and of the mundane and sometimes ugly side of everyday life that does not, or rather, cannot, stop just because the world is in peril. This is a story of everyday life that continues in spite of peril. This story follows the Hesses, an Italian-American family from Phoenixville, Pennsylvania as they struggle through their own personal wars. It follows Wendy, 58 and the mother of the family, as well as her children, Phillip, 33, Carolyn 29, and her daughter Anya, six, Rachael, 21, and Connor, 18, as they navigate the throws of divorce, relocation, miscarriage, disability, and mental health both apart and together, even as a global pandemic wreaks havoc on the world. Mentioned are Wendy’s ex-husband Ron, 58, and her middle son, Ryan, 24, who did not pass through the house during this time period. This story also chronicles a poignant moment in my own personal history. In March 2020, I came to visit Rachael for spring break, as I normally do, only expecting to stay for two weeks. I ended up living with them for five months, finding my own living situation less than favorable. They took me in without hesitation. I have known the Hesse family since 2015, a year after they moved to Phoenixville, but was surprised and deeply moved by their kindness and willingness to support me even through their own strife.


Being given someone else’s soul is a privilege and responsibility that I had never seen as an oppportunity for self-reflection, rather as just a way to hold space. This story changed that. The ability to learn, to grow, to change with a group of people over a year is an experience that is unique to few storytellers, and I am eternally lucky to have been given the chance to experience such intimacy, such trust, and such raw love. Simply stated, I hope it impacts you as much as it has impacted me. These photographs follow the five months I lived with the Hesse family, and the seven months after where I was allowed to be a part of their bubble.


This story was written under mentor by Kia Gregory, for our senior capstone class in the spring 2021 semester. It will become available for pre-order as a physical book in the coming months. “The reason we all talk about each other even though we are so angry is because we’re hurt.

And we’re hurt because in the end, we will always love each other.” - Rachael Hesse



Rachael watching a COVID-19 update from the Pennsylvania Emergency Management Agency on March 30th, taking a break from her classes. Rachael was one of the many college students home on break, delayed indefinitely from her campus at American University in Washington, D.C.
Rachael bleaching her bathtub after a day of deep cleaning. Upon moving back into her high school bedroom, Rachael decided to re-organize, de-clutter, and re-arrange to make the space feel like hers again. She only had two hours to move out of her dorms, making the six-hour round trip drive in one day.

Connor making dinner. Connor had been isolated before quarantine, when his girlfriend broke up with him and cut him out of his friend group. He spent most of his time up in his room, talking to Phillip and Ryan over video games. It wasn’t until Rachael came home that he started to spend more time downstairs, and around Wendy.


The last time Wendy had a house this full of children was 2015, one year after she moved her family from Danbury, CT. Itwas shortly after her eldest son, Kenny, 31, passed away from a heart attack. Carolyn, Anya, Geoffrey, Wendy’s sister Suzy, and her daughter Elise, her ex-husvand Ron, Ryan, Rachael, and Connor were all living in the house together. To say Wendy was prepared would be an understatement. Piles upon piles of non-perishables, perishables yet to be put away, cooling baked goods and prepped dinners dotted the counters. Mountains of dishes arose in the sink, which were a nightly fight between the family, even before quarantine. There were weekly grocery trips, which proved hectic due to mass panic-buying. Wendy’s then-boyfriend, Felix Grau (pictured bottom right) helped with shopping trips - accompanying Wendy and even paying for dinner some nights. With a six year old, a pregnant woman, and three young adults in the house, there was rarely a moment where the kitchen was empty.

Rachael taking a break from online classes to pet Ruby, the family dog. In January of 2020, Rachael injured her back while on the rowing team at school. Thinking it was a muscle strain, she was benched, but otherwise did not change her routine. By the end of February, she could barely bend over to tie her shoes. Because of the shelter-in-place, she was not able to see a physician or get to a hospital to figure out what was wrong, leaving her with Bengay and painkillers as her only option. She spent much of the first few months of quarantine on the couch or in her bed, unable to walk upright.In addition to her physical health, Rachael’s mental health was suffering. At school she had begun getting a diagnosis for her untreated Attention Deficit Disorder (ADD) and chronic depression. She had just been about to receive medication when she came home to Pennsylvania, upon which she found out her psychiatrist was unable to practice outside of D.C., leaving her to start the process over again with a new doctor.


Wendy painting the foyer. Wendy and Ron’s relationship had ended just before their 40 year wedding anniversary. They had been married at 18, when she found out she was pregnant with Kenny. In 2016, Ron wrote her a note, and walked out of the front doors of 471 Springview Lane. Since then, Wendy has been trying to sell the house, but has faced many obstacles - mainly financial ones. She had worked as a middle school teacher for fifteen years, while Ron had worked for the pharmaceutical company Perdue. When he left, she was supporting her five-child family on a teacher’s salary. In January 2020, she received a cease and desist letter from Ron’s lawyer, but due to the housing market and her own desire, decided to stay an extra year in the house.

Anya kissing Carolyn’s pregnant stomach. On New Years Eve 2020, Carolyn found out she was pregnant again, after suffering the miscarriage of her second child the previous September. The night she announced it to Wendy and Rachael was the same night Governor Tom Wolf announced Pennsylvania’s month-long lockdown. Fearing her town in Virginia Beach, VA would become a police state, she remained with Wendy, leaving her husband Geoffrey alone as an essential worker as a commercial truck mechanic.





Wendy calling about an overpriced phone bill as she sorts through various medical papers for Rachael. In addition to selling the house, Wendy was also working on transitioning her middle school classroom online - learning how to use Zoom, pre-record lessons, work remotely, and create lesson plans that worked in an online environment. She often started her work day at 3 a.m.




Anya and Carolyn making a vinegar and baking soda volcano. It became a game among the family of who could keep Anya entertained the longest - a much-needed respite from classes and the news. There were science projects, art projects, cooking experiments, many movie days, picnics, fashion shows, spa nights, and a gigantic fort built in the basement, all with the intention of having fun. Anya was five when quarantine started, turning six in November. Her lack of understanding, and subsequent lack of somberness, lifted everyone’s spirits in the house, and gave them an excuse to let their minds rest from worrying.

Lisa and Roseann Hesse, Ron’s sister and mother, inside Roseann’s house. Wendy had been sending groceries and baked goods over to the house, along with homemade signs from Anya, as Roseann and her husband Albert are over 80, and couldn’t risk leaving the house. Lisa, 57, drove up from Baltimore as soon as she heard Pennsylvania would be on lockdown. She has been living with them ever since.


Family portraits on Easter. Geoffrey was able to travel up to Pennsylvania as soon as restrictions were lifted in April. He had not seen his wife or daughter in almost three months.

Portrait of Anya and Carolyn. The few photos of Carolyn’s pregnant belly were reequested by her, and staged for her comfort. The baby after a miscarriage is called a rianbow baby, because it ‘s birth represents the beauty after the storm of an infant loss. Carolyn was extremely cautious with her body, going so far not share any social media posts about her pregnancy until the baby was born. She was anxious about the prospect of giving birth during a pandemic, and these photos were a way to remember the beauty being brought to life.

Rachael and Wendy on the couch post-Easter. Carolyn and Anya drove back to Virginia, meaning more sleep, but a more somber house, without them.
Felix making lobster for the family. Shortly after this photo was taken, Wendy and Felix’s relationship ended.

Rachael cooing at Gaelan. Gaelan Arthur Miller was born at 8:42 am on September 3rd, 2020, in Virginia Beach, Virginia, happy and healthy with a full head of hair. Carolyn went into an induced labor due to possible complications the week before. But by the time he arrived, the family wept with joy.

Wendy lying with Rachael after her second round of cortisone shots. By August, Rachael had been diagnosed with a herniated disc in her lower back, which had completely shattered - the pieces of which were pressing into her spinal cord. When she was finally able to see a pysician, she was told that the lack of immediate care had caused irreversible damage, leaving her with few options. The first was several rounds of cortisone shots into her spinal cord, and if those didn’t work, major invasive surgery.

Wendy hugging Rachael as they look at her school account, a medical form, and an email from her psychiatrist. Wendy has been supporting Rachael throughout quarantine in any way she can: massaging her back, making doctor’s calls, and wagering with the school to help her receive accomadations. When Wendy and Ron divorced in 2018, the stipulation was that he would cover child support, college tuition for Rachael and Connor, help to sell both their Connecticut house and eventually this one, and file the taxes he had neglected to: which was the ultimate cause of their separation. Wendy had received little to no financial help, making her the sole provider. By the summer, both she and Rachael were working two jobs to support the extra medical bills.

Rachael putting an icepack on her face after a day of vomiting and dizzy spells from her ADD medication.

Portrait of Wendy. During the first few months of quarantine, Wendy decided she needed an additional stream of income, and set up her own business helping families receive governmental money owed to them when their house becomes foreclosed. She takes a percentage of comission for every client. Her first check paid for an entire semester of Rachael’s tuition. In order to stay in the house the extra year, they needed another source of income.

Paint gear on the floor, abandoned for lunch. Wendy’s decision to stay an extra year in the house was one she made with intention. By August 2021, Connor would be out of the house at college, and she would officially be an empty-nester, with no one to take care of for the first time in 37 years.

Connor and Ruby after dinner. Throughout quarantine Connor has helped around the house, often doing dishes, moving boxes, helping with paint, and doing the heavy lifting jobs where Wendy, Rachael, and Carolyn cannot. In April 2021, Connor accepted a spot at the University of Denver in Colorado for college, which gave the family mixed emotions. Colorado is a sore topic in the household. It is the state where Kenny passed away, where Ron left to when the separation first happened, and where Phillip and Ryan, who have only been speaking to Wendy the past two years, are currently living. For Connor, he sees college as a new start, a way to escape his status as “the baby” and make a life for himself that is his own.

Rachael and Connor arguing. These two have bonded the most over the past few years, both witnessing the aftermath of their parent’s divorce together. Rachael is worried about how Wendy will be when everyone leaves again, knowing she will be the sibling in closest proximity, in Washington D.C.

A continuation of the argument. The five siblings’ relationship is complicated, with Phillip and Ryan only speaking to Wendy and Connor the past two years. Carolyn and Rachael have been effectively cut out, or cut themselves out. After Kenny’s death, the siblings made a pact never to completely shut one another out. But over the past few years, that pact has not held.

Phillip sitting alone at the kitchen table. In March 2021, Phillip called Wendy, asking for a plane ticket home from Colorado, where he and Ryan had been living the past two years. He needed to leave his living situation, and had fallen out with Ryan. This is the first time Rachael, Connor, or Wendy had seen him since he left.

Family dinner. The living situation came as a shock. The family decided he could stay: under the conditions that he got a job and attended therapy.

The family talking after dinner. Tensions in the house became high, especially between Rachael and Phillip. It is a hard time navigating the very real consequences of their relationships with one another, knowing they but are unsure how to reconcile with what has happened; some of which may not be able The general consensus is that the family itself is embarking on six individual paths: Phillip Carolyn focusing on her family in Virginia, Ryan on his own in Colorado, Rachael in D.C., Connor beginning college in Colorado, and Wendy’s embarkment into the single, wherever that may take her. The family will not be together in one place again for a very them is hope. Hope that one day, things will get better, that family dinners won’t ache so will be kept. And with it the knowledge that things have already begun to change. Knowledge days are behind them, even for a pandemic. Knowledge that no matter how many times out, no matter how many times the door seems closed, and no matter how bad the going when it is over, even just for a moment, they will celebrate.

ACKNOWLEDGEMENTS

First and foremost I would like to thank the Hesse family, for allowing me into their lives and into their personal moments. You have given me a trust I do not carry lightly.


I would like to thank my mentors, Kia Gregory and Allison Lichter-Joseph, for always encouraging my projects. Your continued support, even if we did not always agree, and insistence to think outside my proverbial lightbox has given me a confidence in my work I never thought was possible.


But mostly I would like to thank you, the named or nameless who have read these words, felt these photos, taken the time to pick up this project and sit with it. The coronavirus pandemic has changed our world for good - the way we interact and have been allowed to see one another has changed. We are in a time of mass grieving, and a dire need of empathy. Empathy is the hardest truth to learn and the most coveted ally once earned. But how we proceed in that empathy is up to us. The world has changed and so have we, who hold the unwitting accountable. It is my hope that this book touched you in some way, whether that be an emotion or a thought. I know the world is bereft of comfort, and there are days when it does not feel worth it to care. I have felt it on most days this year. But we must remember that we are all here together. That to be alive is to seek community. That to care is an act of righteousness. In the days that are coming in the aftermath of this global tragedy, the best we can do is hold each other’s hands. The Hesse family has held mine. Thank you for holding theirs.
